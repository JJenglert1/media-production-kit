## Using This Table

- Match BPM and swing first -- these define the genre's feel more than anything
- Mode/scale determines harmonic color; key feel determines chord voicing style
- Core sounds tell you which instrument builders to prioritize
- Signature effects are what make the genre recognizable -- always include them
- For genres not listed, research the closest match and adjust

## Deep Genre Profiles

The following profiles expand on each genre with sub-genre variants, real reference tracks, chord progressions, drum programming guidance, arrangement templates, mixing philosophy, and common anti-patterns. Use these when you need to go deeper than the quick-reference table above.

### Lo-fi Hip Hop

Sub-genres: chillhop, jazzhop, lo-fi R&B, study beats

Reference tracks (study these):
- Nujabes "Feather" — 82 BPM, Dm, jazz piano sample over boom-bap drums with a loose swing that defines the entire aesthetic; the hi-hats are imperfect and humanized
- J Dilla "Don't Cry" (Donuts) — 78 BPM, Eb minor, the quintessential example of MPC-style swing where quantize is intentionally pushed off-grid; chops come early and late by 10-30ms
- Idealism "Controlla" — 75 BPM, Gm, demonstrates how a single Rhodes loop with vinyl crackle layered underneath creates a full atmosphere with minimal elements

Common chord progressions:
- im7-IV7-VII-VI — classic Dorian vamp; the backbone of jazzhop (used in countless lo-fi playlists)
- IVmaj7-iii7-vi7-IImaj7 — descending major key movement with extended voicings; gives that "warm afternoon" feel
- im9-iv9-VII7-IIImaj9 — minor key jazz cycle; the 9th extensions are critical for that lush, slightly melancholy vibe
- ii7-V7-Imaj7-vi7 — jazz turnaround borrowed directly from bebop; works as a four-bar loop

Drum patterns:
- Kick on beats 1 and 3 (slightly late on 3 by ~20ms); snare on 2 and 4; cross-reference boom-bap patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Hi-hats: eighth notes with swing set between 55-60%; occasional 16th note ghost hits at low velocity (30-50%)
- Kick and snare should feel "loose" — use velocity variation (60-110 range) and micro-timing offsets to emulate MPC finger drumming
- Layer a low-passed vinyl crackle or tape hiss beneath the drum bus to glue everything into that "dusty" aesthetic

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Chorus/Hook: 8 bars | Bridge: 8 bars | Outro: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~48-64 bars at 78 BPM = ~2:30-3:20
- Key transitions: add/remove elements gradually; a simple filter sweep or vinyl stop effect between sections; avoid hard cuts

Mixing philosophy:
- Roll off everything above 12-15kHz with a gentle low-pass filter to achieve that "warm tape" quality. Keep the bass round but not sub-heavy — lo-fi is about midrange warmth, not club impact. Subtle tape saturation on the master bus adds cohesion. Stereo image should be relatively narrow — this music lives in headphones, and a mono-compatible, intimate soundstage is the goal. Light sidechain compression (1-2dB) from kick to keys/pads keeps things breathing without being obvious.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT quantize drums to a rigid grid — the human imperfection IS the genre; perfectly quantized drums instantly sound like stock music
- Do NOT use bright, crispy hi-hats or modern trap hats — they cut through the low-pass aesthetic and break the illusion
- Do NOT over-produce with too many layers; lo-fi is minimalist by nature — 4-6 elements maximum (drums, bass, keys, sample, texture, maybe a lead)
- Do NOT skip the vinyl/tape texture layer — without it, the track sounds like a jazz demo, not lo-fi hip hop

### Synthwave

Sub-genres: outrun, darksynth, dreamwave, retrowave

Reference tracks (study these):
- Kavinsky "Nightcall" — 108 BPM, Fm, iconic gated snare reverb over arpeggiated bass synth; the vocal is vocoder-processed and sits deep in the mix behind the synth layers
- Perturbator "Humans Are Such Easy Prey" — 115 BPM, Cm, darksynth side of the spectrum; heavy distorted leads, aggressive LFO modulation on bass, and a relentless 16th-note arpeggio driving underneath
- FM-84 "Running in the Night" — 104 BPM, Ab major, dreamwave side; lush saw pads with long attack, shimmering chorus, and the snare is pushed back with gated reverb rather than dominating

Common chord progressions:
- i-VI-III-VII — the "quintessential synthwave" progression; minor key but with a hopeful uplift on the VI chord (think "Nightcall")
- i-iv-VI-V — darker variant used heavily in outrun; the iv to VI movement creates that 80s cinematic tension
- I-V-vi-IV — major key dreamwave; borrowed from 80s pop (think "Take On Me" territory)
- i-VII-VI-VII — power chord movement common in darksynth; works with both distorted leads and clean arps

Drum patterns:
- Kick on every quarter note (four-on-the-floor); snare on 2 and 4 with gated reverb tail (~200-300ms); cross-reference electronic-basic patterns
- Hi-hats: straight 8th or 16th notes, no swing; open hat on the "and" of 2 or 4 for accent
- Toms: use deep, reverbed toms for fills — this is the 80s aesthetic; tuned toms descending in pitch are a signature move
- Clap layered with snare is standard; keep the clap slightly behind the snare transient for width

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 8-16 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Chorus/Drop: 16 bars | Bridge: 8 bars | Outro: 8-16 bars
- Total: ~72-88 bars at 110 BPM = ~3:00-4:00
- Key transitions: filter sweeps on the arp, reverse cymbal crashes, snare rolls building into chorus; the "drop" is usually the addition of the lead synth melody rather than a bass drop

Mixing philosophy:
- The mix should feel wide and spacious. Heavy use of stereo chorus on pads and arps to fill the panorama. The bass synth should be mono and anchored center. Gated reverb on the snare is non-negotiable — set a reverb with a noise gate that cuts the tail at ~250ms for that classic Phil Collins/80s sound. Compression should be moderate; let the dynamics breathe but keep the kick punching through the pads via sidechain. High frequencies should shimmer — this is not lo-fi; the top end is bright and present.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use modern EDM supersaws — synthwave saws should sound analog, slightly detuned (2-3 voices, not 7+), and run through chorus/ensemble effects rather than being massively stacked
- Do NOT forget the gated reverb on the snare — a dry snare instantly breaks the retro illusion
- Do NOT use 808 sub bass — synthwave bass is mid-focused (80-300Hz), often a mono saw or square with filter movement
- Do NOT make the arrangement too "busy" — synthwave breathes; leave space for the arpeggio to hypnotize

### Trap

Sub-genres: melodic trap, drill, rage, plugg

Reference tracks (study these):
- Metro Boomin "Bad and Boujee" (feat. Migos) — 127 BPM half-time, C#m, textbook trap: the 808 slides between notes, hi-hats roll in 32nd-note triplets, and the melody is a simple minor key flute pattern looped
- Pi'erre Bourne "Magnolia" (Playboi Carti) — 130 BPM half-time, Dm, minimalist plugg production; the 808 pattern is melodic and carries the track harmonically while the synth lead is sparse and bell-like
- Southside "Savage Mode" (21 Savage) — 132 BPM half-time, Am, dark trap; the kick and 808 are practically fused, the hi-hats have heavy swing in rolls, and ambient pads create a menacing atmosphere beneath the percussion

Common chord progressions:
- im-im-iv-iv — two-chord vamp, extremely common; the simplicity leaves room for the 808 to be melodic
- im-VI-VII-im — minor key with a brief lift; used in melodic trap (Gunna, Lil Baby territory)
- im-v-iv-VI — drill-style dark progression; often played with piano or dark bells
- Single chord drone with 808 melody providing all harmonic movement — common in minimalist plugg

Drum patterns:
- Kick pattern follows the 808 exactly or is layered on top of the 808 transient for click; snare/clap on beat 3 (half-time feel at 130+ BPM); cross-reference trap patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Hi-hats: the defining element — 16th notes as the base with rolls accelerating to 32nd and 64th note triplets; use velocity to create dynamics in the rolls (crescendo into the snare hit)
- Open hat punctuations on off-beats; 808 should have a long decay (1-2 seconds) and glide/portamento between notes
- Percussion: shaker, snap, and rim shot as ghost elements; occasional reverse cymbal for transitions

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Hook/Chorus: 8 bars | Verse 2: 16 bars | Hook: 8 bars | Bridge: 8 bars | Outro: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~64-80 bars at 140 BPM (half-time) = ~2:30-3:30
- Key transitions: 808 drops out briefly before the hook; hi-hat pattern changes density; risers/reverse 808s signal section changes

Mixing philosophy:
- The 808 bass is king — it should dominate the low end (30-80Hz) with heavy distortion/saturation for presence on phone speakers. Sidechain the kick to the 808 so the kick transient cuts through. Hi-hats should be crisp and bright (8-16kHz) but not harsh — a slight roll-off above 16kHz helps. The mix is generally sparse: let each element have its own frequency space. Hard-clip the master for loudness — trap is meant to be loud and aggressive. Vocals (if present) sit on top with heavy autotune and plate reverb.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use a short 808 — the long, sustained, distorted 808 is what defines modern trap; a short bass note sounds like hip hop from 2005
- Do NOT quantize hi-hat rolls to straight 16ths without variation — the triplet rolls and velocity swells are what make trap hi-hats exciting
- Do NOT use acoustic drum sounds — trap is entirely electronic; a real snare or kick breaks the aesthetic immediately
- Do NOT overcrowd the melody — trap beats are sparse; one or two melodic elements maximum, leaving space for vocals

### House

Sub-genres: deep house, tech house, progressive house, future house

Reference tracks (study these):
- Disclosure "Latch" — 122 BPM, Fm, deep house with a UK garage influence; the off-beat chord stabs and a pitched vocal sample over a tight four-on-the-floor kick define it; the bass is a sub with a filtered top layer
- Daft Punk "One More Time" — 123 BPM, Eb major, filtered disco sample chopped and looped with aggressive sidechain compression creating that pumping feel; the arrangement builds by opening the filter gradually
- Eric Prydz "Pjanoo" — 128 BPM, Cm, progressive house; the piano riff is introduced dry and minimal, then layers of pads, risers, and percussion build over 2-3 minutes toward the drop

Common chord progressions:
- im7-iv7 — two-chord deep house vamp; the minor 7ths give warmth, and the off-beat rhythm does the rest (see Disclosure, Kerri Chandler)
- I-vi-IV-V — major key progressive house; bright and uplifting, often with supersaw stabs
- im-VII-VI-V — descending minor; gives an emotional, driving quality common in melodic house
- ii7-V7-I — jazz-influenced turnaround used in deep house and French house (Daft Punk territory)

Drum patterns:
- Kick on every beat (four-on-the-floor is mandatory); clap/snare on 2 and 4; cross-reference house patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Hi-hats: off-beat 8th notes (the off-beat hat is a signature of house); open hat on upbeats for energy
- Percussion: shakers on 16th notes, ride cymbal pinging on quarter notes in breakdown sections, conga/bongo loops for organic texture
- Rimshot or clap ghost notes between main hits add groove without cluttering

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 16 bars | Build: 16 bars | Drop/Chorus: 16-32 bars | Breakdown: 16 bars | Build: 8-16 bars | Drop: 16-32 bars | Outro: 16 bars
- Total: ~128-160 bars at 124 BPM = ~5:00-7:00
- Key transitions: filter sweeps (low-pass opening into drops), snare rolls, white noise risers, removing the kick for 4-8 bars before the drop returns

Mixing philosophy:
- The kick is the foundation — it needs to punch at 50-60Hz with a tight transient click around 3-5kHz. Sidechain compression on bass, pads, and chords from the kick is essential (4-8dB of gain reduction) to create the "pumping" effect. The mix should feel warm in the low-mids, airy on top. Stereo width comes from the chords and pads; keep kick, bass, and clap dead center. For deep house, roll off highs slightly for warmth; for tech house, keep the hats crispy and the percussion forward.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT skip sidechain compression — without the pump, house music loses its rhythmic engine and sounds flat
- Do NOT use a half-time snare pattern — house is always four-on-the-floor with snare/clap on 2 and 4; anything else and it is no longer house
- Do NOT make the intro too short — house tracks are designed for DJ mixing with long intros (16+ bars of just drums); cutting this short makes the track unusable in a set
- Do NOT use a distorted 808 bass — house bass is clean, round, and sub-focused or a warm filtered saw; trap-style 808s do not belong here

### Ambient

Sub-genres: dark ambient, space ambient, drone, ambient techno

Reference tracks (study these):
- Brian Eno "Music for Airports 1/1" — no fixed BPM (tempo is irrelevant), C major, interlocking piano and synth loops of different lengths create ever-shifting patterns; the reverb tail is as important as the notes themselves
- Tim Hecker "Virginal II" — no fixed BPM, atonal/textural, granular processing of source material (piano, guitar) until it becomes unrecognizable; heavy use of saturation and spectral processing to create evolving density
- Stars of the Lid "Requiem for Dying Mothers Pt. 1" — extremely slow, Db major, layered string drones with glacial harmonic movement; each chord lasts 30-60 seconds with micro-tonal variations in the sustain

Common chord progressions:
- Single sustained chord with slow voicing changes — the "progression" happens within one chord as voices shift (Eno's approach)
- I-IV (or Imaj7-IVmaj7) — two chords alternating every 8-16 bars; Lydian color on the IV gives that floating quality
- Imaj7-vi7-IVmaj7 — glacial three-chord cycle; each chord sustains for a full phrase before moving
- No traditional progression — pitch drones with overtone movement; common in dark ambient and drone sub-genres

Drum patterns:
- Most ambient has no drums at all; rhythm comes from pulsing pads, granular textures, or subtle LFO modulation
- Ambient techno variant: extremely sparse kick (every 2 or 4 bars), muted and reverbed, with soft metallic textures as percussion
- If percussion is used, it should sound "found" — field recordings of rain, machinery, or processed clicks rather than traditional drum sounds
- Any rhythmic element should be deeply reverbed and low in the mix; it suggests pulse rather than defines it

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 16-32 bars | Movement A: 32-64 bars | Movement B: 32-64 bars | Movement C: 32-64 bars | Outro: 16-32 bars
- Total: ~128-256 bars at 70 BPM (if metered) = ~7:00-15:00
- Key transitions: crossfade between textures over 8-16 bars; elements emerge and dissolve gradually; no hard cuts, no drops — everything flows

Mixing philosophy:
- Reverb is the primary instrument — use convolution reverbs with 5-10 second decay times, or layer multiple algorithmic reverbs to create an impossibly large space. Dynamic range should be wide; let quiet moments be truly quiet and loud swells fill the spectrum. Compression should be minimal or absent — ambient music needs to breathe. EQ focus: scoop out muddy low-mids (200-400Hz) to maintain clarity in the wash of reverb. The stereo field should be immersive and wide — use mid/side processing to push elements to the edges.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT add drums thinking the track "needs rhythm" — ambient is about texture and space; a standard beat destroys the meditative quality
- Do NOT use short reverb tails — anything under 3 seconds sounds too "present" and earthbound for ambient; the reverb IS the sound
- Do NOT rush harmonic movement — if your chords change every 4 bars, you are writing a ballad, not ambient; sustain each harmonic idea for 16-32 bars minimum
- Do NOT over-compress or limit the master — ambient music relies on dynamic range; crushing it into a loud, flat waveform defeats the entire purpose

### Trance

Sub-genres: uplifting, psytrance, progressive trance, tech trance

Reference tracks (study these):
- Armin van Buuren "Blah Blah Blah" — 138 BPM, Am, classic uplifting structure with a massive supersaw lead appearing after a 2-minute build; the gated pluck arpeggio drives the verse while the kick provides relentless energy
- Infected Mushroom "Becoming Insane" — 145 BPM, Em, psytrance; rapid 16th-note acid bass lines, heavy use of resonant filter sweeps, and the arrangement is structured around psychedelic builds and releases with unpredictable sound design
- Above & Beyond "Sun & Moon" — 134 BPM, Eb major, progressive/vocal trance; long emotional pad swells, a restrained supersaw lead, and the breakdown strips everything to piano and vocals before the triumphant rebuild

Common chord progressions:
- vi-IV-I-V — the "uplifting trance" progression; major key euphoria, used in countless anthem tracks
- i-VII-VI-VII — minor key trance driver; creates urgency and forward motion (common in tech trance)
- i-iv-v-i — natural minor cycle for darker progressive trance; pairs well with minor key supersaw stabs
- i-III-VII-VI — epic minor key progression with a major lift on III; used in vocal trance breakdowns

Drum patterns:
- Kick on every beat (four-on-the-floor, non-negotiable); punchy, clicking kick with presence at 3-6kHz; cross-reference trance patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Off-beat open hi-hat is the standard driving element; closed hat on every 16th note during build sections
- Clap on 2 and 4; layered with a tight snare for definition
- Ride cymbal introduced during peaks for additional energy; crash cymbal on the downbeat of drops

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 16-32 bars | Breakdown 1: 16-32 bars | Build: 8-16 bars | Drop 1: 32 bars | Breakdown 2: 16-32 bars | Build 2: 8-16 bars | Drop 2: 32 bars | Outro: 16-32 bars
- Total: ~160-224 bars at 138 BPM = ~6:00-8:00
- Key transitions: the "trance breakdown" is the genre's emotional core — strip everything to pads and maybe piano, then use a long riser (white noise + snare roll + pitch-rising synth) over 16 bars to build tension before the drop crashes back in

Mixing philosophy:
- The kick needs to be loud and forward with a tight low end (50-70Hz) and a pronounced click. Sidechain everything to it — the pumping effect should be audible and part of the groove. The supersaw lead must be wide (7+ voices, detuned, with stereo spread) and sit prominently in the mix. Reverb should be dramatic: long tails on pads and breakdowns, but kept tight on the kick and bass during drops. The overall mix is bright — trance favors presence in the 2-8kHz range. Master compression should be assertive; trance is meant to be loud and relentless on a club system.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT skip the long intro/outro — trance tracks are built for DJ sets; short intros make them unmixable and DJs will not play them
- Do NOT use weak, thin supersaws — the supersaw lead is the emotional payoff; it needs to be fat, wide, and dominating; a thin saw sounds amateur
- Do NOT neglect the breakdown — a trance track without an emotional breakdown is just monotonous repetition; the contrast between breakdown and drop is the entire point
- Do NOT use swing — trance is rigidly quantized and mechanical; any swing destroys the relentless forward momentum

### Drum & Bass

Sub-genres: liquid, neurofunk, jungle, jump-up

Reference tracks (study these):
- Noisia "Could This Be" — 174 BPM, Fm, neurofunk; the Reese bass is a masterclass in sound design — a detuned saw with heavy processing, distortion, and modulation; the drums are chopped breaks layered with punchy electronic kicks
- Calibre "Even If" — 172 BPM, Cm, liquid DnB; smooth Rhodes chords over a rolling breakbeat with a deep sub bass; the swing in the break gives it a jazzy, flowing quality that defines liquid
- Andy C "Heartbeat Loud" — 176 BPM, Am, dancefloor DnB; the break is chopped and rearranged with surgical precision, the bass switches between a sub and a mid-range growl, and the energy never drops

Common chord progressions:
- im7-iv7 — minimal two-chord vamp; extremely common in liquid DnB; the simplicity lets the bass and drums dominate
- im-VI-III-VII — ascending minor key movement; gives liquid DnB its emotional, uplifting quality
- im-im-iv-v — dark minor cycle for neurofunk; pairs with heavy bass sound design
- Chromatic bass movement (no traditional chord progression) — common in neurofunk where the bass IS the harmonic content, moving in semitones

Drum patterns:
- The "two-step" pattern: kick on 1, snare on the "and" of 2 (i.e., halfway through beat 2); this offset creates the rolling feel; cross-reference breakbeat patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Hi-hats: fast 16th notes with ghost notes and accents creating a complex, flowing pattern; open hat for accents
- Breaks: chopping classic breakbeats (Amen, Think, Apache) is traditional; even modern DnB references these patterns
- Jungle variant: time-stretched and pitch-shifted breaks with heavy processing, creating chaotic, rolling percussion

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 16 bars | Build: 16 bars | Drop 1: 32 bars | Breakdown: 16 bars | Drop 2: 32 bars | Outro: 16 bars
- Total: ~128 bars at 174 BPM = ~3:00-4:00
- Key transitions: the "drop" is dramatic in DnB — strip back to atmospherics, then slam the bass and break back in; use reverse cymbals, snare rolls, and risers; half-time sections provide contrast before double-time returns

Mixing philosophy:
- The drum break needs to be loud, punchy, and compressed hard — it drives everything. Use parallel compression on the break bus for density while keeping transients. The sub bass should be clean and powerful below 80Hz; the mid-bass (Reese) should be heavily processed and sit in the 100-500Hz range with harmonic distortion giving it presence. Sidechain the bass to the kick. For liquid, the mix is warmer and more spacious; for neurofunk, it is tight, loud, and aggressive. Reverb on snare and atmospheric elements, but keep the break relatively dry.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use a four-on-the-floor kick pattern — DnB's identity is the breakbeat; a straight kick kills it
- Do NOT make the bass too clean in neurofunk — the movement, grit, and modulation of the Reese bass is what makes neurofunk neurofunk; a static sine sub is for liquid, not neuro
- Do NOT ignore the swing in the break — even at 174 BPM, the ghost notes and micro-timing in the hi-hats create groove; quantizing everything to a rigid grid makes it stiff
- Do NOT write short intros if the track is meant for DJ play — DnB DJs need 16-bar intros for mixing

### Jazz

Sub-genres: bebop, modal, jazz fusion, smooth jazz, acid jazz

Reference tracks (study these):
- Miles Davis "So What" — 136 BPM, Dm Dorian (shifts to Eb Dorian for the bridge), the quintessential modal jazz track; the bass states the two-chord melody, piano comps with rootless voicings, and the ride cymbal drives with heavy swing
- Herbie Hancock "Chameleon" — 98 BPM, Bbm, jazz-funk/fusion; a Clavinet riff drives over a synth bass line; the drums are tight and funky with ghost notes on the snare; the Fender Rhodes solo section is a masterclass in extended harmony
- Robert Glasper "Afro Blue" — 105 BPM, Fm, modern jazz; blends classic jazz harmony with hip hop drum programming; the Rhodes chords are lush and complex (11ths, 13ths) while the beat underneath nods to J Dilla's swing

Common chord progressions:
- ii7-V7-Imaj7 — the most fundamental jazz progression; appears in virtually every standard
- im7-IV7-im7-V7 — Dorian minor vamp; the basis of modal jazz (Miles Davis "So What" territory)
- Imaj7-vi7-ii7-V7 — "rhythm changes" turnaround; the engine of bebop
- iii7-VI7-ii7-V7 — extended turnaround with secondary dominants; adds harmonic richness and forward motion

Drum patterns:
- Ride cymbal: "spang-a-lang" pattern — quarter notes with a triplet skip beat on the "and" of 2 and 4; this IS jazz drumming
- Hi-hat: foot closes on 2 and 4 for timekeeping while hands are on ride
- Bass drum: "feathering" — very light quarter notes felt more than heard; occasional accents ("dropping bombs") for emphasis
- Snare: ghost notes and brush work define the texture; cross-reference jazz patterns in drum-patterns reference

Arrangement template:
- Head (melody): 32 bars (often AABA form — 8+8+8+8) | Solo section: 32-128 bars (multiple choruses of the form) | Head out: 32 bars | Tag/ending: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~96-200 bars at 120 BPM = ~4:00-8:00
- Key transitions: solos trade between instruments; the rhythm section adjusts intensity behind each soloist; endings often use ritardando or a repeated "tag" phrase

Mixing philosophy:
- Jazz mixing is about capturing a performance in a room. Use room or hall reverb to create a sense of space — all instruments should sound like they are in the same acoustic environment. Minimal compression; dynamic range is sacred in jazz — the difference between a whisper and a shout is part of the expression. EQ should be naturalistic: warm lows, present mids, smooth highs without harshness. The drums should sound like acoustic drums in a room, not processed electronic hits. Stereo image should mirror a stage: piano slightly left, bass center, drums slightly right (or however the ensemble would naturally sit).

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT quantize anything — jazz is about human feel and swing; quantized jazz sounds lifeless and robotic
- Do NOT use simple triads — jazz harmony requires 7th chords minimum; 9ths, 11ths, and 13ths are the standard vocabulary; a plain C major chord has no place in jazz
- Do NOT over-compress the drums — the dynamic range of jazz drumming (ghost notes to accents) is critical; squashing it removes all expression
- Do NOT use electronic/synthesized drum sounds for straight-ahead jazz — acoustic timbres (brushes, sticks on real cymbals) are non-negotiable for authenticity

### Classical

Sub-genres: orchestral, chamber, minimalist, neo-classical

Reference tracks (study these):
- Debussy "Clair de Lune" — rubato tempo (~72 BPM base), Db major, a masterclass in dynamic control and pedaling; the arpeggiated figures flow freely without rigid tempo; the piece moves through harmonic regions with voice leading that is smooth and inevitable
- Philip Glass "Opening" from Glassworks — 96 BPM, steady pulse, A minor/C major, minimalism defined; a repeating arpeggiated pattern in the piano with subtle harmonic shifts every 8-16 bars; the rhythm is metronomic but the harmonic movement creates emotional arc
- Olafur Arnalds "Near Light" — 70 BPM, Eb minor, neo-classical; combines string quartet with subtle electronic textures (granular pads, soft beats); the strings play long sustained lines with intimate close-mic recording

Common chord progressions:
- I-IV-V-I — fundamental tonal progression; the basis of common-practice harmony
- i-iv-V-i — minor key standard; the raised 7th in the V chord (harmonic minor) creates the leading tone
- I-vi-IV-V — Romantic-era progression; emotional and widely used in film scoring
- Repeating arpeggiated patterns over I-V-vi-IV or i-III-VII-VI — minimalist approach (Glass, Einaudi)

Drum patterns:
- Traditional classical has no drum kit; rhythm comes from timpani rolls, orchestral percussion (triangle, cymbals, snare drum in marches), and the rhythmic patterns of the instruments themselves
- Neo-classical may incorporate soft electronic beats: muted kick, delicate hi-hat, processed with heavy reverb to blend with acoustic instruments
- Minimalist: rhythm is generated by repeating patterns (ostinato) in the piano or strings rather than percussion
- If percussion is used, it should support the ensemble, never dominate — a timpani roll under a crescendo, a triangle marking downbeats in a dance movement

Arrangement template:
- Sonata form: Exposition (A theme + B theme): 32-64 bars | Development: 32-64 bars | Recapitulation: 32-64 bars | Coda: 8-16 bars
- Minimalist: Single movement, 64-256 bars of gradual transformation at steady tempo
- Neo-classical: Intro: 8 bars | A section: 16-32 bars | B section: 16-32 bars | A return: 16-32 bars | Outro: 8-16 bars
- Total varies enormously: 3:00 for a short neo-classical piece to 12:00+ for a full sonata movement

Mixing philosophy:
- The goal is to recreate an acoustic space. Use a single high-quality hall reverb to place all instruments in the same room. Dynamic range is paramount — classical music has the widest dynamic range of any genre, from ppp to fff. Do NOT compress the master bus. EQ should enhance the natural timbre of instruments: warmth in cellos (200-400Hz), presence in violins (2-4kHz), air on flutes (8-12kHz). Close-mic for intimacy (neo-classical) or room-mic for concert hall realism (orchestral). The stereo image should follow orchestral seating: first violins left, cellos right, woodwinds center, brass behind.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT compress or limit the master — classical music lives and dies by its dynamics; a loud, squashed classical mix is an abomination
- Do NOT use synthesized instrument sounds without careful programming — bad string samples are immediately obvious; if using samples, invest in expression mapping and realistic articulations
- Do NOT ignore tempo variation — classical music breathes with rubato, rallentando, and accelerando; a metronomic tempo grid works only for minimalism
- Do NOT add reverb to individual instruments separately — they should all share the same acoustic space or the illusion of a real ensemble collapses

### Metal

Sub-genres: thrash, death, black, djent, progressive

Reference tracks (study these):
- Meshuggah "Bleed" — 132 BPM, polymetric (4/4 over implied odd groupings), the kick drum pattern is a relentless 32nd-note figure; guitars are tuned to F and use palm-muted staccato patterns that create rhythmic illusions against the 4/4 time
- Gojira "Stranded" — 114 BPM, Dm, modern progressive metal; heavy palm-muted chugging alternates with open melodic sections; the production is tight and punchy with the kick drum cutting through a wall of distortion
- Tool "Schism" — shifts between 5/8, 7/8, and other odd meters, Em, the bass drives the main riff while guitars layer textural parts above; the arrangement builds from sparse and clean to heavy and distorted over 6+ minutes

Common chord progressions:
- Power chord riffs on i — metal often avoids traditional progressions in favor of single-note or power chord riffs; the rhythm IS the harmony
- i-bII — the Phrygian half-step; the most "metal" interval — E to F power chords (think Metallica, Slayer)
- i-bVII-bVI-V — descending minor with a Phrygian dominant resolution; used in thrash and progressive metal
- Chromatic movement — djent and death metal frequently use chromatic riffs rather than diatonic progressions; the tension is the point

Drum patterns:
- Kick: double bass drum is essential — constant 16th notes (thrash), triggered patterns (death), or syncopated polyrhythmic figures (djent); cross-reference metal patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Snare: on 2 and 4 for standard time; blast beats (alternating snare-kick at 16th or 32nd notes) for extreme metal
- Hi-hat/ride: rides for verse sections, crashes for emphasis, china cymbal for accent hits
- The kick drum must be triggered or sample-replaced for consistency at high speeds; natural kick at 200+ BPM becomes mushy

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse riff: 8-16 bars | Pre-chorus: 4-8 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Verse 2: 8-16 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Bridge/Solo: 16-32 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Outro: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~72-128 bars at 130 BPM = ~3:30-5:30
- Key transitions: tempo changes, meter changes (especially in progressive), breakdowns (half-tempo heavy section), clean-to-distorted dynamic shifts

Mixing philosophy:
- Guitars should be double-tracked (same part, panned hard left and right) for width and thickness; high-pass at 80-100Hz to leave room for bass and kick. The kick drum needs a pronounced click (3-5kHz) to cut through distortion at high speeds — sample replacement or triggering is standard. Bass guitar fills the low-mids (80-300Hz) and can be driven with distortion for presence. Compression is heavy on individual tracks but the mix should still breathe dynamically between soft and heavy sections. The overall mix is dense but every element must remain defined — use surgical EQ cuts to carve frequency pockets.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT neglect the low-end balance between kick, bass, and guitars — if the guitars have too much low end, the mix becomes muddy and the kick disappears; high-pass the guitars
- Do NOT use a clean, unprocessed guitar tone for heavy sections — metal distortion requires specific amp modeling, tight gain staging, and post-EQ to sound powerful rather than fizzy
- Do NOT skip double-tracking rhythm guitars — a single guitar panned center sounds thin and amateur; the wide stereo double-track is fundamental to metal production
- Do NOT program blast beats at tempos where the sample or sound cannot maintain definition — a blast beat that sounds like white noise defeats its purpose

### Chiptune/8-bit

Sub-genres: classic 8-bit, modern chiptune, bitpop

Reference tracks (study these):
- Anamanaguchi "Meow" — 150 BPM, A major, modern chiptune blended with real guitars; the lead is a square wave with fast arpeggiation mimicking the NES sound chip; layered with live drums and bass for a full-band bitpop sound
- Chipzel "Spectra" — 160 BPM, C major, pure Game Boy chiptune; three channels of pulse/wave plus noise for percussion; the rapid arpeggios create the illusion of chords from a single monophonic channel
- Disasterpeace "Forgotten" (FEZ soundtrack) — 110 BPM, Eb major, ambient chiptune; demonstrates that chip sounds can be meditative and atmospheric; simple melodies with long sustains and careful use of the limited channel palette

Common chord progressions:
- I-IV-V-I — straightforward major key; classic game music optimism (think Mario, Mega Man)
- i-III-VII-VI — minor key epic progression; used in boss battle and dramatic game themes
- I-V-vi-IV — pop progression adapted for chip sounds; works in bitpop and modern chiptune
- vi-IV-I-V — emotional variant; common in RPG and adventure game themes

Drum patterns:
- Noise channel percussion: short noise bursts for hi-hat, medium noise for snare, pitched-down short noise or triangle wave kick
- Patterns are simple and clear: kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hi-hat on 8th or 16th notes
- Fills are created with rapid noise bursts or pitch sweeps rather than additional percussion sounds
- The limitation IS the aesthetic — do not add extra percussion channels; the noise channel does everything

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Loop A: 16 bars | Loop B: 16 bars | Loop A variation: 16 bars | Loop C (bridge): 8-16 bars | Final loop: 16 bars
- Total: ~80-96 bars at 140 BPM = ~2:00-3:00
- Key transitions: minimal — chiptune often loops; transitions are marked by a brief silence (1 beat), a pitch sweep, or a drum fill using the noise channel

Mixing philosophy:
- Do NOT add reverb or delay — authentic chiptune is bone-dry because the original hardware had no effects processing. Each channel should be clear and distinct with no frequency overlap masking (the limited channel count makes this natural). The mix should be loud and forward with minimal dynamic range — this mirrors the output of actual sound chips. If blending chip sounds with modern instruments (bitpop), keep the chip elements dry and forward while the live instruments can have standard processing. Stereo width is minimal; original hardware was mono. Modern chiptune can use hard-panning (chip left, chip right) for a faux-stereo effect.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT add reverb or delay to chip sounds — it immediately sounds wrong; the dry, direct sound IS the aesthetic
- Do NOT use more than 3-4 sound channels simultaneously — the charm of chiptune comes from the limitation of hardware sound chips (NES had 2 pulse, 1 triangle, 1 noise); exceeding this sounds over-produced
- Do NOT use complex waveforms (saws with multiple harmonics, FM sounds) for classic 8-bit style — stick to square/pulse, triangle, and noise
- Do NOT over-quantize velocity — while chiptune notes are either on or off (no velocity), varying the pulse width or pitch slightly between notes adds the subtle life that pure mechanical playback lacks

### R&B

Sub-genres: neo-soul, alternative R&B, PBR&B, classic R&B

Reference tracks (study these):
- Frank Ocean "Nights" — 90 BPM (first half, switches midway), Cm, alternative R&B; sparse production with a muffled drum machine, layered vocal harmonies, and a dramatic beat switch halfway through that changes tempo, key, and mood entirely
- SZA "Love Galore" — 68 BPM, Eb minor, modern R&B; the beat is minimal — a soft kick, snare with plate reverb, and a chopped vocal sample; the bass is a deep sub that barely moves; everything serves the vocal
- D'Angelo "Untitled (How Does It Feel)" — 72 BPM, Dm, neo-soul; the drums are played live with extreme swing and ghost notes, the bass (Pino Palladino) is round and melodic, and the Rhodes/Wurlitzer chords are warm with subtle phaser

Common chord progressions:
- im9-IV9 — the neo-soul two-chord vamp; extended voicings with 9ths are mandatory (D'Angelo, Erykah Badu)
- IVmaj7-iii7-vi7-ii7-V7 — descending cycle with extended chords; classic R&B sophistication
- I-V/VII-vi-IV — major key with a bass line walk-down; smooth and radio-friendly
- im7-iv7-VII7-IIImaj7 — minor key with Dorian flavor; lush and melancholy

Drum patterns:
- Kick: soft, round, often tuned low; on beat 1 and the "and" of 2 (syncopated); cross-reference R&B patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Snare: rimshot or muted snare with plate reverb; on 2 and 4 with ghost notes between
- Hi-hats: loose 16th notes with heavy swing (58-62%); slightly behind the beat for that laid-back feel
- Neo-soul: live-feel drums with brush snare; modern R&B: programmed drums with a mix of 808-style and acoustic samples

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse 1: 16 bars | Pre-chorus: 8 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Verse 2: 16 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Bridge: 8 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Outro: 8 bars
- Total: ~80-112 bars at 72 BPM = ~3:30-5:00
- Key transitions: R&B uses subtle transitions — a filter opening, a new instrument entering, or the bass changing its pattern; key changes (up a half step for the final chorus) are a classic R&B move

Mixing philosophy:
- The vocal is the absolute center of the mix — everything else supports it. Bass should be warm and round (sub with a soft top), sitting just below the vocal in prominence. Drums are felt more than heard — muted, compressed, and sitting back in the mix. Keys and pads fill the mid-range with lush stereo width. Use plate reverb on vocals and snare for that classic warmth. Chorus and phaser effects on Rhodes/Wurlitzer are standard. The overall tone is warm: roll off harsh highs above 12kHz and boost the low-mids slightly for body.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT make the drums louder than the vocal — in R&B, the voice leads and the production supports; drum-forward mixes belong in hip hop and EDM
- Do NOT use basic triads — R&B harmony requires 7ths, 9ths, 11ths; a plain Am chord sounds empty and amateur in this context
- Do NOT skip the swing — R&B without swing feels stiff and lifeless; even programmed drums need 58-62% swing to breathe
- Do NOT over-process the bass with distortion — R&B bass should be smooth and round, not gritty or aggressive

### Reggae/Dub

Sub-genres: roots reggae, dub, dancehall, lovers rock

Reference tracks (study these):
- King Tubby "Dub Fi Gwan" — 78 BPM, Am, the blueprint for dub mixing; instruments drop in and out as the mixer sends elements to delay and reverb in real-time; the spring reverb on the snare and the tape delay on vocal fragments are the core of the dub aesthetic
- Lee Scratch Perry "Blackboard Jungle Dub" — 82 BPM, Gm, pioneering dub; heavy bass that dominates the entire frequency spectrum, drums stripped to kick and rim shot with massive reverb, and the mix is an instrument — elements are muted, echoed, and transformed live
- Bob Marley "Stir It Up" — 76 BPM, A major, roots reggae; the classic "skank" guitar on off-beats (beats 2 and 4), walking bass line, and one-drop drum pattern (kick and snare together on beat 3 only) define the genre

Common chord progressions:
- I-IV-V-I — simple major key; the backbone of roots reggae (Bob Marley, Peter Tosh)
- i-IV-i-V — minor key with major IV; gives that bittersweet reggae feel
- I-V-vi-IV — pop-influenced reggae; used in lovers rock and accessible reggae
- i-i-iv-V — minor key one-chord vamp with movement to iv and V; common in dub (the simplicity leaves space for mixing effects)

Drum patterns:
- "One drop": kick and snare hit TOGETHER on beat 3 only; beats 1, 2, and 4 are empty (just hi-hat); this is THE defining reggae pattern; cross-reference reggae patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Hi-hat: straight 8th notes or steady 16th notes; cross-stick/rimshot instead of full snare in many styles
- "Steppers" variant: kick on every beat (four-on-the-floor) for a more driving feel; used in roots and dancehall
- Dub: the drum pattern is the same but processed — heavy spring reverb on the snare, delay throws on individual hits, and the mix drops elements out for dramatic effect

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Chorus: 8 bars | Verse 2: 16 bars | Chorus: 8 bars | Dub breakdown: 16 bars | Verse 3/Chorus: 16 bars | Outro: 8 bars
- Total: ~96-112 bars at 78 BPM = ~4:00-5:30
- Key transitions: dub breakdowns strip the mix to bass and drums with heavy effects; instruments return one by one; transitions are marked by drum fills on the rim shot or a full bar of just hi-hat

Mixing philosophy:
- Bass is ENORMOUS — it should dominate the mix, felt physically in the chest. Use a warm, deep bass with most energy below 100Hz and a touch of harmonic content around 200Hz. The "skank" guitar should be thin and mid-range (300Hz-3kHz) — high-pass it aggressively so it stays out of the bass's territory. Spring reverb on the snare/rimshot is non-negotiable for dub. Tape delay (with feedback) on vocal fragments, guitar chops, and percussion is the dub producer's primary tool. The mix should feel spacious despite the heavy bass — the off-beat guitar and sparse drum pattern leave a lot of rhythmic space. In dub, the mixer IS a performer — automate sends to delay and reverb creatively.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT put the kick on beat 1 with the snare on 2 and 4 — that is rock, not reggae; the one-drop pattern (kick+snare on 3) is the genre's heartbeat
- Do NOT use digital-sounding delay — dub delay should be warm and degrading, like tape echo; each repeat should get darker and more distorted
- Do NOT make the guitar full-frequency — the skank guitar must be thin and percussive; a big, chorused guitar steps all over the bass and destroys the reggae frequency balance
- Do NOT neglect the space between notes — reggae breathes; the rests and off-beats are as important as the notes; filling every gap with sound destroys the groove

### Phonk

Sub-genres: drift phonk, Memphis phonk, house phonk

Reference tracks (study these):
- DJ Smokey "Evil Wayz" — 134 BPM half-time, Cm, Memphis phonk origin; chopped Three 6 Mafia vocal samples over dusty, distorted 808s; the cowbell is the primary melodic percussion element; everything is run through tape saturation until it sounds like a worn-out cassette
- Kordhell "Murder In My Mind" — 136 BPM half-time, Am, drift phonk; aggressive distorted 808, a dark synth melody, and the cowbell pattern drives relentlessly; the mix is intentionally clipping and distorted for aggression
- Soudiere "Slow Down" — 130 BPM half-time, Fm, classic phonk aesthetic; heavy use of pitch-shifted vocal samples from 90s Memphis rap, boom-bap influenced drum patterns with heavy swing, and the entire mix is low-passed and saturated

Common chord progressions:
- im-im-im-im — single chord drone; the harmonic content comes from the 808 melody and vocal samples rather than traditional chord changes
- im-iv-V-im — dark minor cycle; used with detuned synth bells or dark piano
- im-bII-im-bII — Phrygian half-step oscillation; creates that menacing, horror-film tension
- im-VII-VI-V — descending minor; used in more melodic phonk tracks

Drum patterns:
- Cowbell: the signature element — steady 8th notes or a syncopated pattern; sometimes pitched and used melodically; cross-reference phonk patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Kick: follows the 808 pattern or hits on 1 and 3 (half-time feel); should be punchy and blend with the 808 transient
- Snare/clap: on beat 3 (half-time); often layered with a rimshot; hit hard with heavy reverb
- Hi-hats: Memphis-style rolls (32nd notes in bursts) similar to trap but grittier and more lo-fi; less precise than modern trap hats

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse/Loop A: 16 bars | Hook: 8 bars | Verse/Loop B: 16 bars | Hook: 8 bars | Outro: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~56-72 bars at 134 BPM (half-time) = ~2:00-3:00
- Key transitions: phonk tracks are loop-based; transitions happen by dropping the 808 out briefly, introducing a vocal chop, or changing the cowbell pattern; builds are minimal — the drop hits suddenly

Mixing philosophy:
- The entire mix should sound like it was bounced to a worn cassette tape — heavy saturation on the master bus, low-pass filter rolling off above 10-12kHz, and a slight boost in the low-mids for muddiness (yes, the muddiness is intentional). The 808 should be distorted and loud, competing with the cowbell for dominance. Vocal samples are pitched down, filtered, and sit in the midrange like ghostly textures. Do not aim for clarity — phonk is supposed to sound grimy, dark, and degraded. The stereo image is narrow; much of the source material was originally mono.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT make it sound clean and polished — phonk is defined by its lo-fi, degraded quality; a crystal-clear phonk mix is a contradiction
- Do NOT forget the cowbell — it is as essential to phonk as the 808; without it, you have a trap beat with distortion, not phonk
- Do NOT use modern, pristine vocal samples — phonk vocals should sound like they were ripped from a 1995 Memphis cassette tape; pitch-shift them down and filter them heavily
- Do NOT over-complicate the arrangement — phonk is loop-based and hypnotic; complex song structures with bridges and key changes miss the point

### Post-Rock

Sub-genres: shoegaze-adjacent, crescendo-core, post-metal

Reference tracks (study these):
- Explosions in the Sky "Your Hand in Mine" — starts ~100 BPM, builds gradually, E major; the clean guitar tremolo picking is the core texture; the track builds from a single quiet guitar to a massive wall of distorted guitars, bass, and crashing drums over 8 minutes; the dynamic arc IS the song
- Mogwai "Take Me Somewhere Nice" — 90 BPM, G major, demonstrates the quiet-loud-quiet dynamic; whispered vocals (rare for post-rock) give way to heavy distorted guitar walls; the bass is melodic and drives the harmonic movement when guitars are textural
- Godspeed You! Black Emperor "Storm" — variable tempo, Dm, the 20-minute epic; field recordings and spoken word open into a slow orchestral build with violin drones, then erupts into distorted guitar crescendos; multiple movements within a single track

Common chord progressions:
- I-V-vi-IV — simple major key progressions repeated as mantras; the repetition over long periods creates emotional weight through accumulated texture, not harmonic complexity
- vi-IV-I-V — same chords, different starting point; the minor start gives a more melancholy entry
- i-III-VII-IV — minor key with a Dorian IV; used in darker post-rock and post-metal
- Single chord or two-chord drone — common in the most atmospheric sections; a single chord can sustain for 2-4 minutes while textures shift around it

Drum patterns:
- The drums often start sparse — floor tom hits or a simple ride pattern; as the build progresses, the pattern fills out to a full rock beat
- Standard rock beat at the crescendo: kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, ride or crash cymbal on 8th notes
- Fills are dramatic — tom rolls building into cymbal crashes signal section changes and peaks
- Cross-reference rock patterns in drum-patterns reference; the key difference is dynamics — post-rock drummers play extremely softly in quiet sections and explosively at peaks

Arrangement template:
- Movement 1 (quiet): 32-64 bars | Build: 16-32 bars | Climax: 16-32 bars | Movement 2 (quiet): 16-32 bars | Build: 16-32 bars | Final climax: 32-64 bars | Denouement: 16-32 bars
- Total: ~144-288 bars at 100 BPM = ~6:00-12:00+
- Key transitions: post-rock transitions are GRADUAL — new elements enter one at a time over 8-16 bars; the build from quiet to loud is the genre's fundamental technique; sudden stops or drops can be used for dramatic effect but should be rare

Mixing philosophy:
- Dynamic range is everything — the quiet sections should be genuinely quiet (barely audible tremolo guitar, soft toms), and the climaxes should be overwhelming walls of sound. Use long reverb and delay on guitars (2-4 seconds) to create a wash of sound. The distorted guitars at the climax should be layered 2-4 times, panned wide, and drenched in reverb to create that "wall" effect. Bass should anchor the low end with a slightly overdriven tone. Drums need room mics — the natural room sound is essential for the live, expansive feel. Do not over-compress; the journey from quiet to loud must be preserved.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT rush the build — post-rock is patient music; if the climax arrives after 30 seconds, you have written a rock song, not post-rock; let it breathe for 2-4 minutes minimum before reaching full intensity
- Do NOT add vocals (generally) — post-rock is instrumental for a reason; the guitars ARE the voice; if vocals must be present, they should be buried in the mix as texture, not lead
- Do NOT keep the dynamics flat — a post-rock track that stays at the same volume throughout has failed at the genre's core premise; the quiet-to-loud arc is the entire point
- Do NOT use electronic drums or programmed beats — the organic feel of a live drummer (with room mics and cymbal wash) is essential to the genre's emotional impact

### Funk

Sub-genres: p-funk, disco-funk, electro-funk, modern funk

Reference tracks (study these):
- James Brown "Get Up (I Feel Like Being a) Sex Machine" — 112 BPM, Eb, the blueprint; every instrument plays a percussive, interlocking rhythmic part; the guitar is scratching 16th notes on a muted chord, the bass locks with the kick, and the horn stabs land on syncopated accents — the "one" (beat 1) is the most important beat in all of funk
- Parliament "Give Up the Funk (Tear the Roof off the Sucker)" — 108 BPM, Em, P-funk; a massive bass line (Bootsy Collins) drives underneath layers of synths, horns, and vocal chants; the clavinet plays rhythmic chords while the drums keep a tight pocket with ghost notes
- Vulfpeck "Dean Town" — 108 BPM, C major, modern funk; bass-centric (Joe Dart) with a minimal drum groove; the bass plays a melodic line that IS the hook while drums and keys lock into a tight, minimal pattern; recorded with intentionally lo-fi warmth

Common chord progressions:
- Single chord vamp (i7 or I9) — funk often sits on ONE chord for the entire track; the rhythm IS the music (James Brown's entire catalog)
- I9-IV9 — two-chord funk vamp; dominant 9th chords are the funk harmonic staple
- im7-IV7 — Dorian minor funk; the IV7 gives that characteristic lift (Earth, Wind & Fire territory)
- I7-IV7-I7-V7 — 12-bar blues influence adapted to funk; each chord gets the 7th/9th extension treatment

Drum patterns:
- The groove is tight and locked — kick, snare, and hi-hat form an interlocking pattern where the ghost notes on the snare are as important as the main hits
- Kick: syncopated, often hitting the "and" of beats (not just 1 and 3); locks with the bass guitar
- Snare: on 2 and 4 with HEAVY ghost notes on every 16th note between; the ghost notes (played at 20-40% velocity) are what makes funk drums funky
- Hi-hat: 16th notes with accents on upbeats; the open hat on the "and" of 4 is a common signature; cross-reference funk patterns in drum-patterns reference

Arrangement template:
- Intro/vamp: 8-16 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Chorus/hook: 8 bars | Verse 2: 16 bars | Chorus: 8 bars | Bridge/solo: 16-32 bars | Chorus/vamp out: 16-32 bars
- Total: ~96-144 bars at 110 BPM = ~4:00-6:00
- Key transitions: funk transitions are minimal — the groove is continuous; sections change by adding/removing instruments (horns enter for chorus, strip to bass and drums for verse); a classic transition is a drum break (2-4 bars of just drums) before the next section

Mixing philosophy:
- The bass and drums are the center of the mix and must be locked together rhythmically — if the kick and bass do not align, the funk is dead. Use tight compression on the drum bus to glue the kit together and bring up the ghost notes. Bass should be warm and round with presence in the 100-300Hz range and a slight growl from light overdrive. The clavinet should be mid-forward (1-3kHz) with a wah filter for movement. Horns are bright and punchy. Keep the mix punchy and dynamic — funk should make you move, which means the transients need to cut through. Minimal reverb; funk is a "dry" genre — tight, close, and in-your-face.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT ignore "the one" — beat 1 is sacred in funk; James Brown built his entire musical philosophy around it; the downbeat must be felt even if it is implied
- Do NOT play ghost notes too loud — they should be FELT, not heard; if the ghost notes are the same volume as the backbeat, you have a drum roll, not a groove
- Do NOT add long reverb tails — funk is tight and dry; reverb washes out the pocket and makes the groove feel loose and unfocused
- Do NOT quantize everything rigidly — funk needs human feel; the slight push and pull between bass and drums (the "pocket") is where the groove lives; quantizing it flat kills the funk

### Blues

Sub-genres: delta, Chicago, electric, blues-rock

Reference tracks (study these):
- Muddy Waters "Mannish Boy" — 80 BPM, E, Chicago blues; a single-chord vamp driven by the stop-time riff (the band stops, Muddy sings, the band hits the riff again); electric guitar through a cranked tube amp, harmonica, and a shuffle drum beat define the sound
- BB King "The Thrill Is Gone" — 88 BPM, Bm, the definitive minor blues; the string arrangement adds drama while BB's guitar (Lucille) sings with vibrato and bent notes in the minor pentatonic; the shuffle is more subtle here, almost straight
- Gary Clark Jr. "Bright Lights" — 76 BPM, Em, modern blues-rock; heavy overdriven guitar, a driving drum groove, and a raw vocal; the production is modern but the 12-bar form and pentatonic vocabulary are pure blues

Common chord progressions:
- I7-I7-I7-I7 / IV7-IV7-I7-I7 / V7-IV7-I7-V7 — the 12-bar blues; the most important progression in all of popular music; every chord is a dominant 7th
- i-i-i-i / iv-iv-i-i / V7-iv-i-V7 — minor blues (12-bar); used in "The Thrill Is Gone" and countless others
- I7-IV7 turnaround (quick change) — the IV7 appears in bar 2 instead of bar 5; adds early harmonic movement
- I7-V7 — two-chord vamp; used in single-chord boogie blues and Delta blues

Drum patterns:
- The shuffle: triplet-based feel where the middle triplet is silent (long-short, long-short); swing setting of 67-75%; THIS is the blues feel; cross-reference shuffle patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4; hi-hat plays the shuffle pattern (triplet 8ths with the middle note ghosted or omitted)
- Brush playing for quieter blues; sticks for electric blues and blues-rock
- Fills are simple — a snare roll into a cymbal crash, or a floor tom pattern; blues drumming serves the groove, never showing off

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4 bars (often a turnaround) | 12-bar verse: 12 bars | 12-bar verse: 12 bars | Solo (12-bar form): 12-24 bars | 12-bar verse: 12 bars | Outro/turnaround: 4-8 bars
- Total: ~52-72 bars at 80 BPM = ~3:00-4:30
- Key transitions: blues sticks to the 12-bar form; the form itself provides all the structure needed; the turnaround (last 2 bars) propels into the next verse; solos follow the same chord changes as the verse

Mixing philosophy:
- Warmth is everything — the mix should sound like a smoky club at 2 AM. Use warm tube saturation or tape emulation on the master bus. Guitars should have a natural amp overdrive (not modern high-gain distortion) — the tone comes from the amp, not pedals. Room reverb (not plate, not hall) creates the intimate club atmosphere. Bass should be round and supportive, never aggressive. Drums are natural and room-mic-heavy; the kit should sound like one instrument, not individual close-mics. Roll off harsh highs; blues lives in the midrange. The vocal is front and center with minimal processing — maybe a touch of compression and a warm reverb.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use straight (non-shuffle) timing — blues without the shuffle/swing feel is just rock; the triplet feel is fundamental and non-negotiable
- Do NOT use modern high-gain distortion on guitar — blues overdrive is warm and dynamic (you can hear the pick attack and volume knob cleanup); a metal distortion tone destroys the expressive dynamics
- Do NOT over-produce — blues is raw and human; too many layers, too much processing, and too much polish strip the soul out of it; three to five instruments is plenty
- Do NOT neglect bent notes and vibrato in guitar/vocal melodies — these micro-pitch inflections ARE the blues; a melody played with perfect pitch and no bends sounds like pop, not blues

### Techno

Sub-genres: Detroit techno, Berlin/industrial, minimal, acid

Reference tracks (study these):
- Jeff Mills "The Bells" — 132 BPM, atonal/textural, Detroit techno at its purest; a single 909 cowbell pattern loops hypnotically while kick, hi-hat, and clap maintain a rigid four-on-the-floor groove; elements are added and subtracted live over the DJ mixer; the arrangement is a live performance, not a composed structure
- Richie Hawtin "No Way Back" — 130 BPM, minimal techno; the 303 acid bass line is the centerpiece — a squelchy, resonant filter sweep over a repeating pattern; the drums are sparse (kick, hat, clap) and the arrangement evolves by modulating the 303's filter cutoff and resonance over time
- Amelie Lens "Hypra" — 138 BPM, Am (loosely), Berlin/industrial techno; pounding kick, industrial textures (metallic hits, distorted synth stabs), and a driving, aggressive energy; the mix is loud and compressed with the kick dominating

Common chord progressions:
- No traditional chord progression — techno is primarily rhythmic and textural; harmonic content comes from a single bass line note or a pad drone
- Single root note with filter movement — the 303 acid line creates harmonic interest through resonance and accent patterns, not chord changes
- im drone with occasional movement to iv or VII — minimal harmonic motion; used in atmospheric/Detroit techno
- Chromatic or whole-tone bass movement — creates tension without resolving to a key center; common in industrial techno

Drum patterns:
- Kick on every beat (four-on-the-floor); the kick is the most important element — it should be heavy, punchy, and perfectly consistent; cross-reference techno patterns in drum-patterns reference
- Closed hi-hat on off-beat 8th notes (standard) or 16th notes (driving); open hat for accents
- Clap on 2 and 4; often a tight, short clap rather than a reverbed one (contrast with trance)
- Ride cymbal for energy in peak sections; rim shot or clave for syncopated percussion patterns
- Acid techno: the 303 bass line follows a 16th-note pattern with slides and accents — the TB-303's characteristic squelch comes from high resonance on the filter

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 16-32 bars (drums only) | Build: 16-32 bars | Main loop: 32-64 bars | Breakdown: 16 bars | Build: 8-16 bars | Main loop 2: 32-64 bars | Outro: 16-32 bars
- Total: ~160-256 bars at 132 BPM = ~6:00-10:00
- Key transitions: techno evolves through subtraction and addition rather than traditional sections; remove the kick for 4-8 bars, then slam it back; introduce a new percussion element every 16 bars; use filter sweeps to create tension and release

Mixing philosophy:
- The kick must be absolutely clean, punchy, and dominant — center it in the mix with a tight low end (50-60Hz fundamental) and a punchy click (2-5kHz). Sidechain everything to the kick for the pumping effect. The mix should be loud and compressed for club play — the mastering targets -6 to -4 LUFS. Bass elements stay mono; width comes from hi-hats, textures, and atmospheric effects. For minimal techno, leave SPACE — the silence between hits is part of the groove. For industrial, the mix can be denser and more aggressive with distortion on the master bus. Use delay (dotted 8th, 16th) rather than reverb for spatial effects — reverb can wash out the precision of techno.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use swing — techno is mechanical and rigid by design; the robotic precision IS the aesthetic; swing makes it sound like house
- Do NOT add melodic hooks or singable melodies — techno is functional dance music; it should hypnotize through repetition, not distract with catchy tunes
- Do NOT make the intro short — like house, techno requires long intros and outros (16-32 bars of drums) for DJ mixing; a track that jumps straight to the main loop is useless to DJs
- Do NOT vary the kick pattern — the consistent four-on-the-floor kick is the anchor; syncopating or removing kick hits (except in breakdowns) undermines the hypnotic drive

### Future Bass

Sub-genres: kawaii future bass, melodic bass, future bounce

Reference tracks (study these):
- Flume "Never Be Like You" — 100 BPM, Eb major, the defining future bass track; the drop features detuned supersaw chords that swell and pulse with heavy sidechain compression; pitched vocal chops fill the gaps; the bass is a distorted sub that wobbles underneath
- San Holo "Light" — 100 BPM, D major, melodic future bass; filtered guitar samples processed into stuttering, glitchy textures; the supersaw chords are massive but warmer than Flume's harsher sound; the arrangement alternates between quiet, stripped-back verses and explosive drops
- Illenium "Beautiful Creatures" — 150 BPM (half-time at 75 BPM), Ab major, melodic/emotional future bass; the drop is built on supersaw chords with vocal chops and a cinematic build; the second drop adds a heavier bass element; very structured verse-chorus-drop format

Common chord progressions:
- I-V-vi-IV — the pop progression adapted with supersaw voicings; ubiquitous in future bass
- vi-IV-I-V — same chords, minor start; gives a more emotional, bittersweet quality (Illenium territory)
- I-iii-vi-IV — substituting iii for V gives a gentler, less resolved feel; common in kawaii future bass
- IV-V-vi-IV — starting on the IV creates an uplifting, anthem-like quality; used in festival future bass

Drum patterns:
- Half-time feel at the verse (kick on 1, snare on 3); the drop shifts energy by adding off-beat percussion and driving hi-hats
- Hi-hats: 8th or 16th notes during drops; often with a "trappy" roll going into the snare hit; less complex than pure trap but borrowing the vocabulary
- Snare: punchy, layered with a clap and white noise transient for maximum impact on the backbeat
- Sidechain everything to the kick — the pumping effect is more aggressive than in house; 6-10dB of gain reduction on the supersaws; cross-reference future bass patterns in drum-patterns reference

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 8-16 bars | Verse: 16 bars | Build: 8 bars | Drop: 16 bars | Verse 2/Breakdown: 16 bars | Build: 8 bars | Drop 2 (bigger): 16-32 bars | Outro: 8-16 bars
- Total: ~96-128 bars at 150 BPM (half-time 75) = ~3:00-4:00
- Key transitions: the build section is critical — use a riser (white noise sweep + snare roll + pitch-rising synth) over 8 bars; the drop cuts all the build elements and slams in the supersaw chords; the second drop should be MORE — add a heavier bass, additional percussion, or a vocal chop pattern

Mixing philosophy:
- The supersaw chords are the star — they should be wide (hard-pan detuned layers left and right), thick, and heavily sidechained to the kick so they pump rhythmically. Use OTT (Over The Top) multiband compression on the supersaws to bring out harmonics and make them shimmer. The bass should be a separate layer from the saws — a dedicated sub or mid-bass that fills the low end while the saws handle mids and highs. Vocal chops should be pitched, chopped, and processed with reverb and delay as textural elements. The mix should be bright and energetic — boost presence (4-8kHz) and air (12-16kHz). Master compression should be assertive; future bass is loud.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT use thin, dry supersaws — the massive, wide, processed supersaw chord IS future bass; if the saws sound like a single oscillator, the drop will fall flat
- Do NOT skip the sidechain on the saws — the pumping rhythm created by aggressive sidechain compression is what gives future bass its bounce; without it, the drop sounds static
- Do NOT make the drops and verses the same energy level — future bass thrives on contrast; the verse should be intimate and stripped-back so the drop hits like a wall
- Do NOT forget vocal chops — they fill the rhythmic gaps between supersaw hits and give the genre its signature playful, chopped-up character

### Indie/Dream Pop

Sub-genres: shoegaze, indie folk, bedroom pop, dream pop

Reference tracks (study these):
- Beach House "Space Song" — 98 BPM, D major, dream pop defined; the organs/synths create a wash of reverb and chorus while Victoria Legrand's vocals float above; the drums are a simple, muted pattern buried in the mix; the guitar is barely there — just adding texture to the overall wash
- Tame Impala "Feels Like We Only Go Backwards" — 97 BPM, B major, psych/dream pop; heavy use of phaser on everything, layered vocal harmonies drenched in reverb, and a bass line that drives the song while guitars swirl in the background with tremolo and modulation effects
- Clairo "Sofia" — 120 BPM, F major, bedroom pop; recorded with lo-fi warmth (slight distortion, tape character), a simple drum machine beat, Juno-style synth pads, and vocals that are intimate and close-mic'd with light reverb; the charm is in the imperfection

Common chord progressions:
- I-iii-IV-I — dreamy major key movement; the iii chord adds a wistful quality without the predictability of V (Beach House uses this frequently)
- I-V-vi-IV — the ubiquitous pop progression; works beautifully under layers of reverb and texture
- vi-IV-I-V — starting on the minor gives an introspective, slightly sad quality; common in indie folk and bedroom pop
- I-bVII-IV-I — the bVII (Mixolydian) gives a psychedelic, floating quality; heavily used in Tame Impala and shoegaze

Drum patterns:
- Simple and understated: kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4; the drums should sit BACK in the mix, never dominating
- Hi-hat: straight 8th notes, consistent velocity; shaker can substitute for hi-hat in a more organic variant
- Drum machine patterns (bedroom pop): simple, slightly lo-fi drum machine sounds (LinnDrum, 707, or lo-fi samples) with no variation; the consistency is the point
- Shoegaze: the drums are buried under walls of guitar reverb; they provide a pulse that you feel rather than hear distinctly

Arrangement template:
- Intro: 4-8 bars | Verse 1: 16 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Verse 2: 16 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Bridge: 8 bars | Chorus: 8-16 bars | Outro: 8-16 bars
- Total: ~80-112 bars at 100 BPM = ~3:30-5:00
- Key transitions: dream pop transitions are smooth and blurred — elements fade in and out; the chorus might simply be the addition of a vocal harmony or a new synth layer rather than a dramatic change; avoid hard cuts

Mixing philosophy:
- Reverb and modulation effects are the defining sonic signature. Every element should have reverb (plate or hall, 2-4 seconds), and guitars/synths benefit from chorus, phaser, or flanger for movement. The vocal should be present but not too forward — it floats within the mix rather than sitting on top. For shoegaze, guitars are layered 4-8 times with different amp settings, all drenched in reverb and distortion, creating a wall of sound. For bedroom pop, embrace lo-fi warmth: slight tape saturation, imperfect tuning, and a narrow, intimate stereo image. Bass should be warm and round, providing an anchor underneath the wash. High frequencies should be softened — roll off above 12-15kHz for warmth.

What NOT to do:
- Do NOT make the drums prominent in the mix — in dream pop, drums are texture, not the driving force; a loud, punchy drum mix belongs in rock or pop, not here
- Do NOT skip the reverb and modulation — a dry dream pop mix is a contradiction; the wash of reverb and chorus IS the genre's identity
- Do NOT over-polish the production (especially for bedroom pop) — the lo-fi warmth, slight imperfections, and intimate feel are the charm; a slick, radio-ready production destroys the authenticity
- Do NOT use harsh, bright tones — dream pop and indie live in warm, soft timbres; aggressive high-frequency content (crispy hats, bright synths, sibilant vocals) clashes with the genre's dreamy atmosphere
